# Executive One-Pager

Turns a long document, email thread, meeting notes, or pile of bullets into a single-page brief a busy reader can skim in 60 seconds. Optimized for the kind of writing staff actually have to produce: status briefs for the dean, vendor evaluations for committees, project updates for managers, decision memos for leadership.

The output is opinionated: bottom line first, supporting detail second, recommendation explicit when one is warranted. No hedging, no preamble, no "as discussed."

---

## When to use

Triggers:

- "Give me a one-pager"
- "Summarize this for the boss / for leadership / for the dean"
- "Executive brief on [topic]"
- "What's the bottom line"
- "TL;DR this"
- User pastes a long doc, email thread, or notes and asks for any kind of condensed version

---

## Inputs to collect

Ask for whatever isn't already provided:

1. **Source material** — paste the doc, thread, notes, transcript, or rough bullets
2. **Audience** — who reads this? (dean, ITAC, manager, faculty, board, public)
3. **Purpose** — inform, recommend, request a decision, or escalate?
4. **Constraint** — any specifics they need to hit (budget number, deadline, named decision)

If only the source material is provided, infer audience and purpose from the content and confirm before drafting.

---

## When the source isn't substantial enough

If the source material is sparse — three sentences, a single email, a thin fragment — **don't synthesize a four-section brief from it.** Inventing context, recommendations, and "open questions" that didn't exist in the source is the failure mode that ends with leadership reading a memo full of fabricated facts.

Instead:

1. Restate what the source actually says, faithfully.
2. Identify what's missing for a real one-pager (audience? recommendation context? data?) — usually 2–3 things.
3. Ask the user to provide the missing pieces, or offer a smaller deliverable: a "what we know vs. what we'd need" note that's honest about the gap.
4. **If the source is internally contradictory or unclear**, surface that explicitly — don't paper over it to produce a clean-looking brief.

A one-pager built on inferred context is worse than admitting the source isn't enough.

---

## Output template

```
# [Title — concrete, ≤ 8 words]
[Audience] · [Date] · [Author / unit]

## Bottom line
[ONE sentence. The single most important thing the reader needs to know. If the reader stops here, they should still have the answer.]

## Context
[2–3 sentences. Why this exists, what triggered it, what's at stake.]

## Key points
- [Most important fact or finding]
- [Second]
- [Third]
- [Fourth — only if essential. Stop at four.]

## Recommendation
[1–2 sentences. State the recommended action and the reason in plain terms. Omit if the brief is purely informational, but say so explicitly: "No decision required."]

## Open questions / next steps
- [Question or next step + owner + (when possible) date]
- [Question or next step]

## Source / for more detail (optional)
[Where the underlying material lives — thread, ticket, doc reference — if the reader might want to dig in]
```

Total length target: **300–400 words.** If the brief is creeping past 500, cut.

---

## Style rules

- **Lead with the answer.** No "Background" section before the bottom line.
- **Active voice.** "We recommend X" beats "It is recommended that X."
- **Specific over vague.** "$12,400 over budget" beats "exceeds budget materially."
- **Numbers, dates, names.** Cite them. Don't say "later this spring" — say "May 11."
- **One acronym = define on first use** if there's any chance the audience doesn't know it.
- **No filler phrases.** Cut "as you know," "needless to say," "it should be noted that," "moving forward."
- **No emoji** in the brief itself, even if the source material has them.
- **Bullets, not paragraphs**, for the Key Points and Open Questions sections.
- **Interpretive language matches audience.** Phrases like "signaled," "implied," "effectively agreed" are OK in candid briefs to a manager who reads between the lines. For formal audiences (committees, leadership beyond your manager, board, public), replace interpretation with literal facts — what was actually said, written, or decided.

---

## Audience adjustments

| Audience | Adjustment |
|----------|-----------|
| **Dean / executive leadership** | Strategic framing. Lead with risk/opportunity. Money and timelines explicit. |
| **Cross-school committees** | Neutral institutional voice. Trade-offs spelled out. Decision criteria stated. |
| **Direct manager** | Slightly more candid. Flag your gut take. OK to use "I recommend" rather than "we recommend." |
| **Faculty** | Lead with what changes for *them*. Pedagogical / research framing. |
| **Public / external** | Plain language, no internal acronyms, no internal politics. |
| **Board / advisory** | Strategic and concise. No operational weeds. |

---

## What to do with messy source material

- **Long thread with side-tangents** — keep only what bears on the bottom line. A thread can have 20 messages and resolve to 5 bullets.
- **Verbose original doc** — strip qualifiers, preambles, and "background" sections that the audience already knows.
- **Conflicting information in the source** — surface the conflict in Open Questions; don't paper over it.
- **Missing information** — list it in Open Questions so the reader knows what's not yet known. Do not invent facts to fill gaps.

## Example

### Source material (the user pastes this)

> Hey team, wanted to give a long update on the May 6 AI sessions. We've been working on this for about three weeks now with Mark and Susan. The branding skill is looking good but Marcoms still has to review it before we can call it ready for staff. They've been busy with admissions stuff so the timeline is tight. We also wanted to add a second skill to share that day — accessibility was one option but Mark thought it was too technical. Now we're thinking maybe a daily planner, or a meeting prep helper, or a one-pager generator. We have to pick by Friday so I have time to test before next week. Budget-wise we're fine, no extra ask. Risk is that if Marcoms doesn't review by Wednesday we may only have one skill ready to share, which would be a softer launch than I want. Susan also asked about whether we should record the sessions for staff who can't attend live — I think yes but we'd need to plan around the room recording setup at Garrett.

### One-pager

```
# May 6 AI Sessions — Status & Decision Needed
For: Mark · April 29, 2026 · IT

## Bottom line
On track to launch May 6 with one fully-vetted skill (branding, pending Marcoms by 4/30) and one of three candidate "useful" skills (planner, meeting prep, or one-pager) — final pick needed by Friday 5/2 to leave testing time.

## Context
Three weeks of work has produced one Marcoms-blockable skill (branding) and three plausible second skills aimed at non-technical staff. Mark's feedback ruled out accessibility as too technical for general audience. Marcoms has been heads-down on admissions; their bandwidth is the schedule risk.

## Key points
- Branding skill ready for Marcoms; review must complete by Wed 4/30 to keep launch posture.
- Three candidate second skills tested and viable: daily planner, meeting prep, executive one-pager.
- Budget: no incremental ask.
- Susan flagged that recording the sessions would expand reach; requires Garrett A/V coordination.

## Recommendation
Pick the second skill by Friday 5/2 (preference: one-pager — broadest applicability across roles). Confirm Garrett recording with A/V early next week so we don't miss the window.

## Open questions / next steps
- Marcoms — branding review complete by Wed 4/30 (Ben to follow up Tuesday).
- Mark — pick second skill by Fri 5/2.
- Susan — confirm Garrett A/V recording by Mon 5/5.
```

## Example — no decision required

Sometimes the audience needs to know what was decided, not decide themselves. Lead the Recommendation section with "No decision required" and use it to state the brief's purpose.

### One-pager

```
# Batten student portal access — process for new cohorts
For: Academic Operations leadership · April 29, 2026 · Batten IT

## Bottom line
Process and timeline for giving new students Batten portal access ahead of the August 1 transition from Slate are now defined. The May 8 access target for the current cohort is on track.

## Context
With student communication moving from Slate to Batten systems on August 1, Academic Operations and Batten IT worked through who gets access, how, and when. The process is partly automated, partly manual; this brief documents what was agreed.

## Key points
- Listservs are automated. Group membership refreshes nightly from SIS via Grouper. Once a student is in SIS, they appear in the right lists by the next day.
- Portal access is manual. Academic Ops provides a list of computing IDs and intended groups; Batten IT provisions access on request.
- Minors need a workaround. Because they aren't in SIS until they declare a major, automated groups won't pick them up. Academic Ops will be granted manage access to the Academics group to handle minor adds and removals directly.
- Manual entries require manual cleanup. Anyone added by hand must be removed by hand when their status changes.

## Recommendation
No decision required — process is set. Documenting here so Academic Operations leadership has the same picture as the IT and Academic Ops teams.

## Open questions / next steps
- Academic Ops — send computing ID + group list to Batten IT — for May 8 access target.
- Batten IT — provision access; enable Academic Ops manage access on the Academics group.
- Both teams — establish a light cadence for reviewing manual adds (quarterly?) so cleanup doesn't drift.
```

---

## What this does NOT do

- **Invent facts** — if a number, date, or name isn't in the source, ask for it or list it as an open question. Never make one up.
- **Soften your assessment to be polite** — if the recommendation is "stop doing this," say so. Word it professionally; do not water it down.
- **Replace the writer's judgment** — the user is responsible for the final brief. This produces a strong draft, not the final.

---

## Privacy

Source material — strategy, personnel, budget — is sensitive. Keep it in the response only. Do not echo to logs or external tools.
